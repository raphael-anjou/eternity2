For the people who want to actually solve it. Everyone who has attacked
Eternity II took one of nine roads, from modelling why it resists, through
pruning the search and building boards up, to throwing hardware at the wall.
Pick the road you care about; each opens onto everything the wiki knows about
it, drawn from the theory, the solver-building guides, and the lab notebook
alike.

## The record, and the people behind it

The wiki also keeps the community's memory: who did the work, how the record
climbed, and the reference shelf you check your own code against.

<div className="not-prose grid gap-3 md:grid-cols-3">
  <Door to="/research/people" title="Who's who">
    The people behind two decades of Eternity II research (founders,
    record-holders, theorists), with what each contributed and the posts to
    prove it.
  </Door>
  <Door to="/research/history" title="History">
    A scannable timeline of the turning points, from the mailing list founded
    in 2000 to the 470 record that still stands, each linking into the full
    account.
  </Door>
  <Door to="/research/records" title="Records">
    The record timeline and the notable boards behind it: who reached 470/480,
    and what those boards reveal about the wall.
  </Door>
  <Door to="/research/papers" title="Papers">
    The academic literature on Eternity II and edge-matching, ranked by how
    useful it actually is to a solver-builder.
  </Door>
  <Door to="/research/reference" title="Reference">
    The exact counts (pieces, colours, edges) to check your own edge-matching
    and constraint code against.
  </Door>
  <Door to="/research/glossary" title="Glossary">
    Every term the wiki leans on, defined once: community jargon, the loaded
    computer-science words, and the notation the boards are written in.
  </Door>
  <Door to="/research/contribute" title="Contribute">
    How to add to the wiki: the sources it trusts, how findings are credited,
    and where to send what you know.
  </Door>
</div>
