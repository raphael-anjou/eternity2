How the solvers that hold the records actually search. They are all, at heart,
depth-first backtrackers; what separates them is the order they try things and
how they bend the rules near the end.

## Plain backtracking

Fill the board cell by cell in some fixed order. At each cell, try every piece
and rotation whose edges match what's already placed; if none fit, back up and
try the previous cell differently. Correct but slow: the search tree is
astronomically wide.

Here is exactly that, in slow motion: a real backtracker on a 3×3, one
decision at a time. Step through it and watch a piece go down, a dead end
appear, and the search un-place and try again.

## Order matters: the fill path

The order you visit cells changes the difficulty by orders of magnitude,
because some orders force conflicts to surface early (good) and others defer
them until a lot of work is wasted (bad). Border-first beats row-major by a
wide margin on the same puzzle.

## Blackwood: schedule and break-index

Joshua Blackwood's solver, the one behind the community's best boards, adds
two ideas to plain backtracking. A heuristic schedule pre-commits to placing a
few high-frequency edge colors on a set timetable by depth, pruning branches
that fall behind. And a break-index allowance lets a handful of fixed late
positions carry one mismatch instead of demanding a perfect match, which is
what makes a 470 (ten mismatches) reachable at all.

## McGavin: raw throughput

The first 469 board came from Peter McGavin running Blackwood's solver
as released, "a few days on about a couple of hundred cores"
([msg 10045](https://groups.io/g/eternity2/message/10045)). No new idea over
Blackwood's schedule, just parallel scale aimed at the right parameter set. His
own auto-generated C engines are a separate story of raw throughput, told in
[McGavin's engine](/research/build/solvers/mcgavin).
The standing 470 record (Blackwood, 2021) came out of the same machinery:
a retuned schedule, not a new algorithm.
