<Callout kind="note" title="Whose work this is">
  The method and the eii solver are **Louis Verhaard's**. This page, written up by
  [Raphaël Anjou](/research/people/raphael-anjou), records what that method was
  and why the original binary cannot be run here. The runnable engine is a
  [reimplementation](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/engines/verhaard-reimpl),
  and it is filed under Raphaël's section because that code is not Verhaard's.
</Callout>

Louis Verhaard is the only person the puzzle ever paid: his eii solver found the
467 that won the $10,000 scrutiny prize in 2008. Unlike McGavin and Blackwood,
he never released source. This page is about that gap, and about what it means to
run "Verhaard" at all.

## Why there is no original to run

What Verhaard shipped is `eii-1.0-win32.zip` from
[shortestpath.se/eii](https://www.shortestpath.se/eii/eii_details.html): a
Windows `eii.exe`, a readme, and some `.bat` files. There are **zero source
files**, and it is Win32 only. It does not run on Apple silicon, and there is no
Windows or emulation layer on this machine to run it under. His artifact is,
here, unrunnable, and there is nothing of his to fetch and build. The only thing
to link is his own page.

## What the method was

Verhaard described his approach on the mailing list: **set-composition
swap-annealing** under a 2x2-tiling metric. In short, pick a roughly
180-piece subset of the interior, swap-anneal its composition until the number
of achievable 2x2 sub-tilings is locally maximised, front-load the pieces that
performed worst, and search the rest under that scaffold. It is a local-search
method for choosing *which* pieces to commit to, not a raw backtracker.

Its numeric constants survive only inside `eii.exe`: the comb-search fill orders
and depth-gated edge slipping that became community canon were recovered
byte-exact from the strings in the binary, because that is the only record of
them that exists.

## Running it means a reimplementation

Because the source is gone, the only way to run his approach is to rebuild it.
That is what the
[Verhaard reimplementation](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/engines/verhaard-reimpl)
does, and on the real five-clue puzzle it reaches 438 of 480, single core. That
engine is filed under Raphaël's section, not this one, because it is a reading of
Verhaard's method, not Verhaard's code. Naming that distinction is part of the
finding: of the three community engines run here, his is the one that cannot be
run at all.
