Give every solver the same budget, one core and one minute, and which one wins?
The answer overturns the obvious intuition. Fifteen solvers, ours and the
community's (Blackwood, Verhaard), each ran once on ten corner-pinned variants of
the official puzzle, single-threaded, 60 seconds a run. Every board was re-scored
by one canonical scorer; no engine's self-reported score is trusted. The maximum
possible score is 480.

## Throughput does not equal score

The Blackwood backtracker, the algorithm behind the community's record, explores
**40.6 million** search-nodes per second. Our beam producer explores **273
thousand** beam-nodes per second, roughly 150 times fewer. On a single core for
one minute, the producer reaches **456** and Blackwood reaches **436**.

Blackwood explores 149 times more nodes per second and still finishes 20 points
lower. At one core the win comes from node quality, not node count: the beam
front-loads its intelligence (comb fill-order, edge-match greedy, a wide
frontier) where the backtracker relies on brute persistence. This is measured
proof of the compute-regime split. Our efficient engine dominates at small
scale; Blackwood only wins with a farm behind it.

## Reading the table

The producer is remarkably consistent, 453 to 456 on every variant with a
standard deviation of 1.0. It wins on skill, not luck. The CSP engines below the
top four are bimodal: on a friendly corner arrangement they reach 340 to 349, on
a hostile one they collapse to about 55, because the arc-consistency search gets
pinned in a bad basin and cannot escape inside 60 seconds. That fragility is the
real story of the lower half of the table.

Throughput units differ by family and are never cross-compared. The producer
counts beam-nodes per second, the backtrackers search-nodes per second, ALNS
counts repair iterations per second. The CSP engines sit at 5 to 10 thousand
nodes per second because each node runs full arc-consistency; they trade
throughput for pruning, which is why they explore far fewer nodes yet still beat
naive DFS on good variants.

## What each family is doing

- **Producer (beam, rank 1).** Wide-beam constructive search over the comb fill
  order (Verhaard and Max's high-score geometry). It keeps thousands of partial
  boards and extends each by the best edge-matching pieces. Low nodes per second,
  but every node is a near-complete, high-quality board. This is the base of our
  record pipeline.
- **ALNS (rank 2).** Two stages inside the 60 seconds: the producer makes a base
  board, then large-neighbourhood local search (destroy a region, repair it)
  polishes it. At 60 seconds it manages only about 32 repair iterations, so it
  lands just under the raw producer here; the real 461 record needed about 20
  minutes of ALNS.
- **Verhaard and Blackwood (backtrackers, ranks 3 to 4).** Depth-gated-break DFS
  at 20 to 40 million nodes per second. They punch to 437 to 451 but hit a wall:
  the endgame needs far more compute than 60 seconds allows. Blackwood's record
  took roughly 180 core-years.
- **CSP with AC-3 (ranks 6 to 15).** Arc-consistency propagation makes each node
  expensive but well-pruned. Strong on friendly variants, fragile on hostile
  ones. Three of them (`gacolor_ac3`, `gacolor_ac3_ns1`, `verhaard_preferred`)
  produced byte-identical boards on this puzzle: NS-1 and preferred ordering add
  nothing here at this depth.
- **Naive DFS (rank 5).** Fills the whole board allowing every break. It reaches
  a high matched-edge count (365) but a low-quality board full of breaks. Fast,
  and junk.

## Where these numbers sit

This grid caps every engine at one core for 60 seconds, far below the compute
that produced the records below. It measures per-core efficiency and heuristic
quality, not peak reachable score. An engine that ranks high here reaches good
boards cheaply; the record numbers need many cores times hours.

| reference | score | conditions |
|:--|--:|:--|
| Community all-hints ceiling | 470 | Blackwood, farm-scale (about 180 core-years) |
| Community 5-hint record | 464 | Pejic and benj39100, 2026 |
| Our verified record | 461 | comb-producer then ALNS, about 20 min |
| This grid's best (producer) | 456 | one core, 60 s |
| Blackwood on our 8-core hardware | 438 | proves 464 to 470 is a compute wall |

## Method and reproducibility

Each of the ten variants is the official puzzle plus three pinned corner cells
(distinct corner-piece arrangements), so all ten share the 256-piece set and 5
clue hints but differ in three corner constraints. They are emitted as both
site-schema JSON (for the native engines) and CSV (for the standalone engines)
from one generator, so every algorithm sees identical instances. One run per
puzzle, fixed seed; the corner arrangement is the only diversity axis. Every run
emits a bucas `.url`, and the score is the canonical matched-edge count from the
same scorer, never the engine's self-report. There were zero failures across all
150 runs.

Every one of the fifteen solvers is open source and runs from this repository.
The crate workspace, the ten variants, the committed per-run results and the grid
scripts all live under the experiment's
[backing directory](https://github.com/raphael-anjou/eternity2/tree/main/research/experiments/raphael-anjou/single-core-benchmark),
and `just raphael-anjou single-core-benchmark` builds the engines and reruns the
whole grid. The native family (ranks 5 to 15) is one binary selected by preset;
the four strong engines (producer, ALNS, Verhaard, Blackwood, ranks 1 to 4) are a
binary each, driven by a small wrapper. Both speak the same puzzle-in,
bucas-url-out contract, and every board is re-scored by the one canonical scorer.

## Companion findings

Profiling put 96.6 percent of the CSP family's time in one heavily pre-optimised
AC-3 loop, and three bit-identical optimisation attempts on the producer's hot
path all measured neutral or worse. These engines are already at their
performance ceiling; the benchmark speeds are their real speeds, not an
implementation gap. Separately, the community's Blackwood constants do not
transfer across colour labelings: its published privileged colours scored 387 in
our labeling versus 435 for a re-fit, a 48-point gap that our Blackwood closes by
re-fitting only the labeling-relative colour IDs while matching every structural
element of the published spec.
