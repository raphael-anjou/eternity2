The CSP presets are not one algorithm but one engine wearing different heads. A
single constraint-propagation core does the search; a preset chooses which
variable-ordering, which value-ordering, and which propagators it runs with. The
point of keeping them is measurement: each preset is a faithful reimplementation
of a known community technique, so its exact cost and reach can be read off the
[single-core benchmark](/research/lab/experiments/single-core-benchmark) against
the constructive engines rather than argued about.

Unlike the constructive engines that drive the records, these are not where the
records come from. They are the baselines: what the classic constraint-solving
approach actually buys on this puzzle, at one core, in one minute.

## The propagators and orderings

Every preset shares the same backtracking core and differs only in what it plugs
into it:

- **gacolor** enforces a colour-graph matching condition, a per-colour
  generalisation of arc-consistency in the style of Régin's alldifferent
  propagator, so a partial board that cannot possibly balance its remaining
  colours is pruned early. Variants (`ns1`, `lcv`, `random`) change only the
  value ordering on top of the same propagator.
- **joe** is a prune-and-restart schedule in the Joe-Saunders style: propagate
  to a fixed depth, and restart when the search wedges rather than grinding on a
  bad line.
- **verhaard_preferred** uses [Verhaard's
  2008](/research/build/solvers/verhaard-eii) preferred-piece value ordering, the
  same idea the community's eii solver used, measured here in isolation.
- **border-first** presets fix the border ring before the interior, in
  least-constraining-value, full, or random orders.
- **rare-colour-first** orders by scarcest colour, binding the tightest
  constraints soonest.

The general theory behind all of this, arc-consistency, AC-3 and its
refinements, is on the [arc-consistency
page](/research/build/reduce/arc-consistency); the [fill-order
page](/research/build/backtracking/fill-order) covers the ordering choices.
These presets are those ideas as built and timed here.

## The naive floor

Two more presets set the floor: plain depth-first search in row-major and in
spiral visit order, with no propagation at all. They exist to answer "what
does the puzzle give you for free," and the answer is instructive: row-major
naive DFS reaches a high matched-edge count on a low-quality board full of
breaks, while the same search in spiral order collapses. The visit order alone
moves the result by hundreds of points, which is the whole argument for the comb
order the producer uses.

## What they show

On the benchmark the CSP presets are bimodal: strong on a friendly corner
arrangement, fragile on a hostile one, because arc-consistency search can get
pinned in a bad basin and cannot escape inside the budget. That fragility, set
beside the producer's flat consistency, is the measured case for constructive
search over classic constraint solving on this puzzle, which is exactly why
these presets are documented as baselines, not as the record engines.
