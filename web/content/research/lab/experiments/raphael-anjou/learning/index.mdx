Most experiments in this notebook search from first principles: a beam builds, a
backtracker digs, a repair loop polishes, each reasoning only from the rules of the
puzzle. The five gathered here do the opposite. They read the corpus of strong
boards the community and this project have already found, mine it for structure, and
feed that structure back into a search as a bias. It is closer to imitation learning
than to search design, and it is a recurring enough pattern to deserve one home. The
method-agnostic write-up of each technique lives in the theory section on
[learning from strong boards](/research/build/learning); this page is the lab side,
the five runs that embody it, with their boards and their numbers.

## The five, and what each learns

| Experiment | What it learns from the corpus | How the search uses it | Reached |
|---|---|---|---|
| [PRIOR](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/runs/prior) | where each piece tends to sit in strong boards | beam-construction tiebreak | 460 |
| [KEYRING](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/runs/keyring) | three ranking signals (position, adjacency, patch), voted | beam-construction ranking | 460 |
| [LODESTONE](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/runs/lodestone) | which pieces serve a scarce colour demand | construction tiebreak (a faint compass) | 451 |
| [PALIMPSEST](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/runs/palimpsest) | the shared bad habits that cap strong boards | ALNS destroy aimed at the traps | 463 |
| [REPLAY](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/analyses/replay) | the exact move a record board used | constrained DFS replay | 460 |

They line up with the theory pages one to one. [PRIOR](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/runs/prior)
and [LODESTONE](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/runs/lodestone) are
[corpus priors](/research/build/learning/corpus-priors), one over positions and one
over scarce demands. [KEYRING](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/runs/keyring)
is [learned move-ordering](/research/build/learning/learned-value-ordering), several
signals voting so no one hunch owns the choice.
[PALIMPSEST](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/runs/palimpsest) is
[anti-pattern mining](/research/build/learning/anti-pattern-mining), and it produced
this project's best board at 463 by separating the structure strong boards share from
the trap they share. [REPLAY](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/analyses/replay)
is [decoding records](/research/build/learning/decoding-records): it rebuilt the
community's strict-460 boards exactly and, in doing so, found the double-break move an
ordinary search could not make.

## The wall all five reach

The through-line matters more than any single score. Every one of these signals is
safe only as a gentle bias, and over-trusting it collapses the search, LODESTONE
falls from 451 to 380 as its weight climbs, and PALIMPSEST's trap list helps as a
steer but hurts as a ban. And even used perfectly, none of them raises the ceiling:
they reach the top of a search's own range reliably and stop, because the corpus they
learn from is made of boards that all hit the same
[rigidity wall](/research/why/rigidity-wall). Learning from strong boards is the
fastest way *to* the plateau and, on its own, no way *past* it. That failure mode is
the point of the [collapse capstone](/research/build/learning/when-learning-collapses),
and it is what makes this a study rather than a list of tricks.

These are five runs from the wider [experiments notebook](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou);
their two structural siblings, the [DFS study](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/dfs-study)
and the [repair study](/research/lab/experiments/raphael-anjou/repair-study), take a
single search paradigm apart one decision at a time instead of learning from the
corpus.
