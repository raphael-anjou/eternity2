Eternity II is not accidentally difficult. This is the science of why no
search, however clever, has reached the end: the puzzle's design, and the
structural walls that show up once you start measuring.

## Where to start

- **[Which wall stops which method](/research/why/walls-and-methods)** lines
  every method up against the wall it attacks and the score where that wall
  stopped it. The one-page map, and the place to orient first.
- **[The rigidity wall](/research/why/rigidity-wall)** is the wall almost every
  method ends against: records are locally frozen islands with no gradient to a
  better board.
- **[The hardness peak](/research/why/phase-transition)** explains why the piece
  and colour counts sit exactly where search is worst.
- **[No forced moves](/research/why/no-forced-moves)** is the wall you feel
  first: every interior cell keeps dozens of legal pieces, so the search never
  narrows on its own.

If you came here asking whether the board is
[NP-complete, and how to encode it](/research/why/how-hard-is-this-instance),
start there: edge matching is NP-complete as a family, but one fixed 16×16
instance is a constant, not a problem.

## Engineered to resist cleverness

Eternity I fell in 2000 because Alex Selby and Oliver Riordan discovered the
puzzle had vastly more solutions than its designer believed, and aimed their
search at the most "solution-dense" regions. For Eternity II, the publisher
hired the winners: Selby and Riordan helped design and stress-test the new
puzzle so that no such statistical shortcut survives.

The visible fingerprints of that vetting: a single designed solution baked
into balanced color counts, no rotationally-symmetric pieces, no duplicate
pieces, and piece-count/color-count parameters sitting at the empirical
hardness peak (later confirmed by Ansótegui et al.). The puzzle isn't
accidentally hard. It was tuned to be.

One thing that looks like a design choice but isn't: the border uses its own
set of five motifs, separate from the interior's. That separation is automatic,
not engineered. Because the outer rim is solid grey, every border piece has its
grey edge fixed outward, so its coloured edges only ever meet other border
edges (sideways) or the interior (inward), and the two pools never touch. The
border motifs could be any five colours, even a relabelling of interior ones,
without changing the puzzle at all. They read as "rare" only because there are
fewer border edges to colour, not because the designers confined a scarce
resource to the frame to thwart solvers. (Thanks to Vasily V. on the
groups.io list for the correction.)

## The structural walls

Beyond the design story, the puzzle has measurable structure that explains
the gap between the best known board (470/480) and a full solution, published
below with the exact computation behind each wall.

One useful way to hold all of this together is to read
[a full board as a codeword](/research/why/permutation-code-wall): its 480
internal joints are parity-like checks, the score is 480 minus the number that
fail, and the use-each-piece-once rule becomes a permutation code layered on
top of the colour checks. The lens renames the score rather than adding a
number, but it puts the two hard constraints in one frame, and the counting
underneath it reproduces exactly. In the same algebraic key,
[flux invariants](/research/why/flux-invariants) weight each edge color and read
every piece as a signed vector: the interior seams cancel, the whole board sums
to zero, and a quarter turn multiplies the vector by the imaginary unit, so the
law is full complex rank 22 on the official set and every bit of it is invisible
to color counting.

Some of these walls are the same constraint seen at different scales.
[Piece theft](/research/why/piece-theft), where one scarce piece spent early
starves a cell rows later, is the cell-level face of the
[area law](/research/why/entropy-area-law): both are the use-each-piece-once
rule, the quiet second rule that carries the hardness, one felt as a single
dead cell and one measured as the collapse of genuinely distinct boards past a
patch of low hundreds of cells. Seen at the scale of a whole fill, the same
coupling produces
[a hard region you cannot design away](/research/why/irreducible-hard-region):
fill the board in any fixed order and the difficulty collects in whichever band
you finish last, so a decomposition relocates the hardness rather than removing
it. [Forbidden patterns](/research/why/forbidden-patterns)
sit alongside on a different axis: they are an exact count of how fast small
patches run out of legal arrangements under colour matching alone, while the
area law's collapse is the separate use-once overlay on top of colour-valid
patches. The two are consistent exact scarcity counts, not the same axis, and
the pages keep them apart on purpose. Whether the deeper causal links hold,
whether rigidity is itself a consequence of the distinctness collapse it shares
a hundred-odd-cell length scale with, and whether the sigma-cycle geometry is what caps
constructive search, are open questions on the
[open problems](/research/open-problems) board, not claims made here.
