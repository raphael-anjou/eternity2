# Digest 0011 — 2011-03 → 2011-12
Coverage: 240 messages, msg_num 8765–9004.

Window context: the first post-contest year (the final scrutiny date passed
2010-12-31; the prize is dead). Traffic collapses to ~24 messages/month and
whole weeks go silent, but the list quietly reorganizes around three things
that replace the money: (1) Brendan's benchmark puzzles as a shared ladder
(9x9 exhaustively searched for the first time, 10x10 becomes "the monster"),
(2) parity/checkerboard balance as the favourite theory problem, and (3)
post-mortems — people publishing what they tried, what failed, and what they
think of Monckton now. Brendan Owen posts his most candid statements ever
about the puzzle's construction. doc_s_smith, the dominant voice of 2010, is
absent all window (Günter asks him to write a review article, msg 8766, and
gets none). No new verified records; the official site confirms the prize
went unclaimed.

### 2011-03-15 — David Munjak's post-mortem: an OR/optimization attack that never backtracked
- who: David Munjak; juraj.pivovarov; Johannes Lindé
- what: Munjak publishes a rare full retrospective of a non-backtracking campaign: convex optimization, Euclidean distance matrices / LMIs, taxicab geometries, molecular-conformation methods, feasibility pump, network flow, simplex, and finally a side-constrained assignment problem whose fractional solution he read as placement probabilities, placing the most probable piece and re-solving — no backtracking at all. ### 2011-05-30 — Lindström's restore-list pruning: a group debugging saga
- who: lindstrom.michael (Michael Lindström); Peter McGavin; Joseph DeVincentis; capiman26061973; Wendy; nc9201
- what: Lindström (the 2008 transposition-table experimenter) describes a backtracker where each field keeps a live candidate list and a piece refuted at field B is REMOVED from B's list and parked on a "restore list" attached to the earlier field whose clearing will make it valid again — a cascade that cuts node counts but silently loses solutions (his 8x8 run misses some of the 52). Over 30+ messages McGavin constructs counterexamples, and Joseph DeVincentis supplies the fix: each field needs multiple restore lists, one per neighbouring edge plus one for pieces used elsewhere (msgs 8871/8886). Lindström measures 6x6 border-ring iteration counts falling 133,326,155 → 30,496,808 (msg 8875) and later finds a logic error making the scheme partially work (msg 8881). The thread is the archive's most detailed public dissection of memoized backtracking's correctness pitfalls.
- source: msg_num 8854 — https://groups.io/g/eternity2/message/8854 — "Alternate approaches - Forum Sleeping..." (DeVincentis fix 8871/8886, counts 8875, McGavin analysis 8866/8868)
- wiki-relevance: technique:pruning-memoization

### 2011-06-07 → 2011-10-16 — The parity mega-thread: checkerboard + rotation-balanced piece sets
- who: juraj.pivovarov; mrjohngilbert (John Gilbert); Varga Laszlo; Peter McGavin; Michael Field; nc9201 (Nick); Jason Jamison; lp0rc; leenstrab
- what: Juraj re-poses the "oriented set" problem — split the 256 pieces into checkerboard piles A/B with every colour's directional edge-counts balanced (equivalently per Bruce: per-colour sums equal pile-to-pile), since knowing orientations OR placements makes E2 trivial (msg 8898). Findings accumulate: Varga's genetic algorithm finds rotation-balanced sets "in a few seconds" while backtracking fails (msg 8900); McGavin reduces the condition to sum(left)=sum(right), sum(top)=sum(bottom) per colour (msg 8906); Juraj estimates ~4.5E485 checkerboard-rotation-balanced sets exist (after Field corrects his first count, msgs 8929–8931) yet finding even one is nearly impossible — he frames it as a hard-parameter PARTITION instance, citing "The easiest hard problem" literature (msg 8907). John Gilbert supplies the negative experimental result: balanced checkerboard sets are findable one-design-at-a-time, but feeding them to a backtracker makes it stall FASTER (half the candidate pieces per placement; fixed-rotation sets a quarter), so restriction costs more than it prunes (msg 8913). Field estimates only 1-in-160,000 random bipartitions can even tile all four 3x3 corners (msg 8911).

### 2011-09-28 — "Playing odds": random possibility-deletion portfolios, tried and sunk
- who: lp0rc; Michael Field; quarter pander; juraj.pivovarov
- what: lp0rc proposes randomly deleting candidate (piece, rotation, position) possibilities from the most-populated cells — each deletion is individually >90% safe, and running 3 solvers with independent deletions gives ~99.9% odds one survives with the true solution intact.
