# Digest 0006 — 2008-11 → 2009-02
Coverage: 440 messages, msg_num 6128–6623.

Window context: this window spans the first scrutiny date (2008-12-31). The
headline arc: pre-deadline hush → TOMY's total silence for two weeks → the
$10,000 runner-up prize surfacing quietly on the UK website (awarded to
Anna Karlsson of Lund — Louis Verhaard's household, score 467) → renewed
post-deadline enthusiasm, a newcomer influx, and a theory shift toward
modelling edge slipping.

### 2008-11-01 — Transposition-table experiments: memorizing unsolvable board states
- who: lindstrom.michael (Michael Lindström); Max; louis.verhaard; migacr
- what: Michael reports coding a scheme that stores visited/unsolvable board states (initial estimate 1.37 GB just for edge information, later corrected: 3 bits×2 for border + 5 bits×14 for interior pieces at the test line). Max and Louis converge on the chess-style answer: fixed memory budget, hash function, overwrite entries. By December Michael reports a refined version that needs no per-piece state, allows the "test point" almost anywhere, uses tiny memory and discards 75–80% of branches on average.
- source: msg_num 6138 — https://groups.io/g/eternity2/message/6138 — "Possible scanline optimization" (hash-table advice 6144, corrected sizes 6145, learning-rate results 6152, improved method 6218)
- wiki-relevance: technique:transposition-tables

### 2008-11-02 — Hybrid fill order proposed: two increasing squares, then scanline
- who: falconmidas
- what: Building on the known result that increasing squares beats scanline early but loses in the mid-depth node peak, falconmidas proposes filling the left half of the board with two increasing squares (bottom-left and top-left) and switching to scanline at 128 pieces, before the 160–170 node-count peak. Framed explicitly as pure optimization, not a route to a solution.
- source: msg_num 6142 — https://groups.io/g/eternity2/message/6142 — "Possible scanline optimization"

### 2009-01-21 — Counting high partials: Max's rule-of-thumb formula for N slipped edges
- who: Max; Michael Field (mfield/Hamster); Brendan Owen
- what: Prompted by "how many 476/477/478 solutions?", Max derives S(480−N) ≈ 2^(N−1)·C(420,N)·S(480) for interior slips, giving ~4.7×10^9 478-partials, ~2.9×10^30 468s, ~1.8×10^32 467s, and a ~60x cost step from 467 to 468 — roughly matching the observed behaviour of Louis's solver. Brendan's older rough estimate was ~100x per extra edge and he lists why edge-slip difficulty estimation needs more than solution counts (where to start slipping, heuristics exploitation, search-order changes).
- source: msg_num 6390 — https://groups.io/g/eternity2/message/6390 — "How many >= 468 solutions?" (question 6367/6389, Brendan 6391)
- wiki-relevance: technique:partial-count, technique:edge-slipping

### 2009-01-24 — Brendan extends complex theory to edge slipping
- who: Brendan Owen; Max; louis.verhaard
- what: Brendan posts the modification of his complex theory (msg 5287) to model edge slipping: a recursion pm(m,v) = pm(m−1,v) − pm(m,v+1) for the probability that exactly v of m joins are valid, combined with C(positions, slips) placement counts. His first table: relative to one 480 solution, 13 slips (score 467) admit 2.05×10^35 times more solutions. Louis — "sorry, can't help it, I am still lurking!" — contributes the outline of his own machinery: a Markov chain over (depth, slips used) built from empirical fit/half-fit probabilities, used to optimize the eii search order and slip array. Max starts integrating slips into his complex-theory program.
- source: msg_num 6408 — https://groups.io/g/eternity2/message/6408 — "Complex theory with edge slip" (table 6412, Louis's algorithm 6423, clarification 6426, Max 6410/6429)
- wiki-relevance: technique:complex-theory, technique:edge-slipping
