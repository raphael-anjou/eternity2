# The rare colors live on the frame

> **Created:** 2026-06-22 · **Updated:** 2026-06-22

## Summary

Eternity II uses 22 colors, but they don't play equal roles. Five of them appear
only along the border ring of the board, each on exactly 24 edges, and never once
in the interior. The other seventeen do almost all their work inside. That clean
split, with the rare colors fenced off to the frame in perfectly balanced amounts,
is one of the clearest fingerprints of a puzzle that was designed, not generated by
accident.

## Method

Every piece has a color on each of its four sides. We sort the colors by where
those sides live. A corner piece has two grey rim edges, an edge piece has one, an
interior piece has none. For each non-grey color we count how many of its edges sit
on a frame piece (edge or corner) versus an interior piece.

## Results

Five colors come out frame-only:

| | |
|---|---|
| Rare colors | 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 |
| Edges per rare color | exactly 24, all on the frame |
| Interior appearances | 0 |
| Total rare edges | 120 |

The remaining seventeen colors each appear on 48 to 50 edges, the large majority
in the interior. So the colors fall into two clean classes: five rare ones nailed
to the border, and seventeen common ones that fill the inside.

The exactness matters. Each rare color appears the same number of times, 24, and
all 120 rare-color edges sit on the 60-piece border. That is not what a careless
generator produces; it is balance imposed on purpose.

## Why it matters

This is design you can see. When Eternity I fell, it was because its pieces had
exploitable statistical regularities. For Eternity II the colors were balanced
deliberately to remove those handholds, and the rare-color fence is part of that
work: it keeps the scarce colors away from the vast interior, where an attacker
would most want a rare, highly-constraining signal to latch onto. The interior is
left to the seventeen common colors, which is exactly why interior-focused search
has so little to grip (see [no forced moves](no-forced-moves)). The border, by
contrast, is where the rare colors make matches tight, which is why every
strong board gets its border essentially perfect.

## Notes

- "Frame" means edge and corner pieces (those with at least one grey rim edge);
  "interior" means the 196 pieces with no grey edge.
- A handful of the common colors do appear a few times on the frame too; only the
  five rare colors are strictly frame-only.
